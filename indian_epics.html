The Great Epic of India

Mahabharata

‡§Æ‡§π‡§æ‡§≠‡§æ‡§∞‡§§

With 100,000 couplets, the Mahabharata is the longest epic poem ever written‚Äî ten times the length of the Iliad and Odyssey combined. Composed by Vyasa around 400 BCE, it tells the story of the Kurukshetra War between the Pandavas and Kauravas.

Yet it is far more than a war story. Within its pages lies the Bhagavad Gita, the sacred dialogue between Arjuna and Krishna that forms the spiritual foundation of Hindu philosophy. It explores duty (dharma), righteousness, and the nature of reality.

The epic begins with the conflict between two sets of cousins over the throne of Hastinapura. What follows is a complex narrative of love, betrayal, divine intervention, and ultimate liberation.

The Ideal Man's Journey

Ramayana

‡§∞‡§æ‡§Æ‡§æ‡§Ø‡§£

The Ramayana, composed by Valmiki, tells the story of Prince Rama‚Äîthe ideal man, the perfect king, the devoted husband. When the demon king Ravana abducts his wife Sita, Rama embarks on an epic journey to rescue her, aided by an army of monkeys and bears.

Unlike the Mahabharata's complexity, the Ramayana presents clear moral dichotomies. Rama represents dharma (righteousness), while Ravana represents adharma (unrighteousness). Yet even Ravana is portrayed with depth‚Äîa scholar, a devotee of Shiva, a king who fell to his own ego.

The epic has been retold countless times across Asia‚Äîin Thailand's Ramakien, Indonesia's Kakawin Ramayana, and the Philippines' Maharadia Lawana. It remains a living tradition performed annually in the Ramlila.
